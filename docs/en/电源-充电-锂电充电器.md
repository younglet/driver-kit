# Lithium Battery Charger


## 1. Module Overview

Lithium Battery Charger is the circuit used to charge lithium batteries. Common application areas include:

- Single-cell Li-Ion charging (4.2 V)
- Dual-cell Li-Ion charging (8.4 V)
- Power banks
- EV battery-management systems (BMS)
- Solar charge controllers
- Wireless charging
- Laptop / phone internal chargers
- Emergency charging
- DIY power banks

Common charger types:

- **Linear charger** (LDO style): simple, low current
- **Switching charger** (SMPS style): high efficiency, high current
- **Synchronous switching charger**: even higher efficiency
- **Wireless charger** (Qi standard)
- **PD trigger ICs**: take power from Type-C
- **Dedicated BMS** (with protection board)
- **Solar MPPT charge controller**


## 2. Working Principles and Use Cases

### 2.1 Charging phases

Lithium battery charging proceeds through four phases:

1. **Trickle charge**: when the battery is below 3 V, apply a small current (~0.1 C) to wake it up
2. **Constant current (CC)**: charge with a steady current (typically 0.5 C ~ 1 C) until the voltage reaches 4.2 V
3. **Constant voltage (CV)**: hold the voltage at 4.2 V (4.35 V for LiHV) while the current tapers down
4. **Charge termination**: stop charging when the current drops below the configured threshold (typically 0.1 C)

### 2.2 Per-cell charging voltages

- **Standard LiPo / Li-ion**: 4.2 V (±1%)
- **LiHV (high-voltage)**: 4.35 V (±1%)
- **LiFePO4**: 3.65 V (±1%)
- An end-of-charge tolerance worse than ±50 mV reduces cycle life

### 2.3 Multi-cell series stacks

- 2 cells: 8.4 V
- 3 cells: 12.6 V
- 4 cells: 16.8 V
- Each cell must be managed individually (to prevent over-charge / over-discharge)
- A BMS (protection) board is mandatory

## 5. Hardware Connection and Electrical Notes

### 5.1 Basic charger circuit

- Input supply (USB / DC)
- Input capacitor (10 µF ~ 100 µF)
- Charging IC
- Output to battery (BAT)
- Output capacitor (10 µF)
- **PROG pin resistor** sets the charge current
- **CHRG / STDBY** status outputs (LED indicator)
- **CE / EN** enable
- **TEMP** temperature protection (NTC 10 kΩ resistor)

### 5.2 PROG pin sets current

```text
R_PROG = 1000 / I_charge(mA)  # kΩ
e.g. 500 mA → R_PROG = 2 kΩ
e.g. 1000 mA → R_PROG = 1 kΩ
```

### 5.3 Protection

- Input TVS for surge protection
- Reverse-battery protection (P-FET or diode)
- Input over-voltage protection
- Charging temperature protection (NTC)
- Heat dissipation (high-current applications)

### 5.4 Power path management

- Some ICs support charge-while-discharging (powering the system simultaneously)
- E.g. LTC4067 / IP5310
- Advantage: plug-in-and-use, seamless switchover when the supply is removed.

Charge time estimation:

```text
Charge time = battery capacity / charge current × 1.3 (efficiency factor)
e.g. 1000 mAh / 500 mA = 2 h × 1.3 = 2.6 h
```

Temperature:

- 10 kΩ NTC attached to the cell
- Charge temperature range: 0 ~ 45 °C (charge), -20 ~ 60 °C (discharge)
- Stop charging on over-temperature (cell safety)


## 6. Communication Method or Control Signal

- Most charger ICs need no communication
- Status outputs: CHRG / STDBY / PG pins
- I2C: some TI BQ parts (e.g. BQ25896)
- SMBus: smart batteries (some laptops)
- 1-Wire: battery ID identification


## 7. Initialization Flow

1. Configure GPIOs (CHRG / STDBY inputs)
2. Configure LED indicators
3. Plug in supply → charging starts automatically
4. Detect battery voltage to determine the charging phase

## 10. Error Handling and Exception Recovery

- **Charging doesn't start**: supply not connected, CE pulled low, TEMP alert
- **Wrong charge current**: wrong `R_PROG`
- **Hot charger**: insufficient cooling — reduce current
- **Won't reach full**: damaged cell, faulty charger
- **Charge jitter**: supply ripple, insufficient filtering
- **Swollen cell**: over-charge protection failed — take out of service immediately

Safety notes — **lithium batteries are dangerous**: over-charge, short circuits and high temperature can cause fire or explosion.

- A BMS protection board is mandatory
- Never leave batteries charging unattended
- For storage, hold the cell at 3.7 ~ 3.85 V (best storage state)
- For transport, keep SOC around 30%
- Extinguish lithium fires with a specialized extinguisher (sand or Class D); never use water
- Store damaged cells separately


## 11. Low Power

- Charger IC standby current is at the µA level
- Disable LED indicator for additional savings
- Disable charging when not needed (via the EN pin)

## 13. Debugging Methods

1. Measure input voltage
2. Measure BAT voltage
3. Measure charge current (multimeter in series)
4. Check LED states
5. Verify PROG resistor value
6. Temperature test (NTC)


## 14. Frequently Asked Questions

- **Slow charging**: charge current set too low
- **Excessive heat**: insufficient cooling — reduce current
- **End voltage too high**: wrong `R_PROG` or charger tolerance
- **Not charging**: supply not connected, battery reversed, CE pulled low
- **Still LEDs lit after full**: internal charger issue
- **Swollen cell**: stop using immediately — over-charge or quality issue
- **PD not working**: wrong trigger IC wiring, protocol mismatch

## 16. Multi-Cell Battery Management (BMS)

- **Over-charge protection**: stop charging above 4.25 V
- **Over-discharge protection**: stop discharge below 2.5 V
- **Over-current protection**: cut off above the rated current
- **Short-circuit protection**: disconnect immediately
- **Balancing**: equalize the voltage of each cell in a stack
- **SOC estimation**: via coulomb counting or voltage
